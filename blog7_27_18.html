On Windy Heights

I often find great comfort in intellectual activity. Oh, it is plenty daring, yes, and has great danger (danger being always the invitation to delight)--but there rests too a security in its logic, its trust in cleanly thought, how it dwells in a realm that is if not orderly then orderable, understanding if not understandable. (Logic is as mystical as iridescent water.) When faced with worrisome or downright despairing things, one may remove oneself to those steady intellectual cliffs and from there observe circumstance with the same tools, attitude, and method with which one might consider a dear friend’s difficulty. “I cannot fix this,” intellection lets us say, “indeed, I barely hope to influence it -- but I can work to understand.”

Yes, this may sound like retreat, evasion, cowardice. Often, I suppose, the intellectual has been levied with that charge. It may sound cold and cruel, too, when she seems to stand amid potent suffering and fear and does not move. Of course there is a time for action, and of course those who in that time do not act with appropriate courage are guilty as charged. However: thinking is also an act, let us not forget, and many moments call for it. World will be always in need of a witness. And thinking is so often a prerequisite for influential action. Without it, our efforts may be futile or even regressive; without it, we may unknowingly commit the same errors and harms that our well-meaning impulses seek to address.

Thinking, then, possesses a humility that understands this likelihood. Thinking likewise stems from a deep compassion which seeks, always, to understand, and, from that place of understanding, to empathize with as much as to heal. Impulsive and self-righteous action, when it is based on denigration and difference as so often it is, casts the demonized other beyond the possibility of understanding. But the premise of all thinking is I can understand you. Or, at least, it is worthwhile that I try. Isn’t this love? This thought-work provides the grounds of relation: it clears the field of action for empathy to take root and for reconciliation and the hope of harmony to flourish.

Put simply: thinking transforms the thinker as much as her thought. While this may not, in the end, be our purpose for thinking, it may be thinking’s purpose for us.

Ah, at last we see it: thinking, while it may appear to remove the thinker to some far-off windy cliffs, instead brings her into direct relation with the world she would study. Inherently reflexive, her thinking involves herself in her study, and as she seeks to understand her subject, her project requires her to pare away all falsehoods and misunderstandings that she herself has brought to the field. Put simply: thinking transforms the thinker as much as her thought. While this may not, in the end, be our purpose for thinking, it may be thinking’s purpose for us.
